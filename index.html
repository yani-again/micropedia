Welcome To MicroPedia!

This is owned and maintained by @yani-again. I'm a 1st year university student in Computer Science and I became heavily interested in embedded systems during my most recent semester.

However, as I tried to learn (first on a Raspberry Pi Pico and now an STM32), I found the available resources had 4 main inconsistencies with how I like to learn:

  • Many resources were TOO basic; I'm someone who likes to understand things down to the transistors, not just to code hobbyist Arduino projects (nothing wrong with that, just not my style).
  • Many were also HAL-only. Seldom would I find something that explained bare-metal programming.
  • Resources were never a one-stop solution. You'd always have to jump between 5 different tabs to get the big picture.
  • My biggest issue: it all assumed decent prior knowledge. What do you mean you won't explain what UART oversampling is and Google turns up no coherent results either?

So, I made my own!

Every article in MicroPedia tries to answer one core question: “what did I need to learn before I felt confident that I understood this topic?”

This site also exclusively focuses on the STM32 family of chips for now because that's what I'm learning right now. The concepts will likely largely apply to other families that use Cortex-M processors (though not entirely), and they will likely largely NOT apply to non-Cortex-M chip MCUs.

I also want to clarify this isn't a complete reference manual or something designed to replace credible sources, MicroPedia focuses on the concepts, assumptions, mistakes, and insights that helped me learn bare-metal.

If you're new to embedded systems and want to understand what's happening beneath the abstractions, I hope these notes help you learn a little faster than I did.

Also, one of the most enjoyable parts of learning about microcontrollers for me is how it maps to regular computer knowledge - for example, the multi-GHz speeds your modern laptop can achieve are achieved in the exact same way that the STM32 achieves a 100 MHz speed through a base clock and a PLL. If you like these kinds of "Eureka!" moments, then you'll love Micropedia!

NOTE! I'm still a university student who's very much learning, so mistakes will definitely happen. If you spot one, have a suggestion, or just extra context to add, please let me know at yanislavyankov23@gmail.com.
